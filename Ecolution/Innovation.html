Innovative Technologies Revolutionizing Waste Management

The first step toward creating efficient, sustainable waste management systems is learning why our current ones aren’t working. The following technologies combine IoT data analytics with modern solutions to help identify challenges and improve as they go.

Zhenjiang is the first Chinese city to treat food waste and sludge in one plant

Home to three million people, Zhenjiang was dealing with the same issue faced by many cities around the world: How to best handle waste? Zhenjiang has a booming restaurant and catering industry with around 2,600 food service businesses that generate approximately 203 tonnes of food waste every day. Faced with large amounts of food waste, as well as domestic sludge from a growing urban population, the city initiated China’s first project for comprehensive treatment of organic urban waste in one plant.

Næstved is recycling glass while producing heat and insulation material

In the southern part of the Danish island of Zealand, Næstved municipality has taken the circular economy to heart. By establishing Resource City, Næstved is working to reduce and recycle waste. One of the ways it recycles waste is via its Glass Cluster, where two companies work together to recycle 125,000 tonnes of glass every year. The cluster not only produces new glass, but also creates insulation from byproduct glass and supplies a local city with 50% of its heating from surplus heat.

Innovation turns waste product into clean water in Bangalore

In Bangalore, a leachate treatment plant is using a new patented method of turning harmful and contaminated leachate into water, which will be used to replenish groundwater reserves. The project saves the city money compared to conventional methods of treating leachate, reduces the risk of contaminating water for the communities surrounding the landfill, and protects the environment from harmful pollutants and emissions. The project is already being expanded across the city and a local government grant is enabling more leachate treatment plants.

Composting waste in São Paulo to boost the circular economy

The City of São Paulo has tackled its problem of sending tonnes of organic waste to distant landfills by constructing semi-local composting facilities in the city. The composting facilities or ‘yards’ handle up to 50 tonnes of waste a day. They create compost to be used in public spaces in the city or given to visitors for free, as well as diverting waste from landfills. The project also includes urban garden programmes in which citizens are encouraged to grow their own food.

Paris is reducing, reusing and recovering its waste

The City of Paris has adopted a zero waste strategy and aims to become a more circular economy. The plan for sending as little waste to incineration as possible includes strategies to reduce, reuse, and recycle and recover waste. Paris has begun extensive collection of organic waste, opened citywide ‘ressourceries’ teaching Parisians how to repair things, and rolled out public recycling bins to enable everyone to recycle regardless of where they live, for example. The city also aims to cut food waste in half by 2025, and is implementing initiatives such as requiring food markets to develop partnerships with nonprofits to redistribute unsold food.

Recycling Apps

Tech startups seem to have have disintermediated almost every aspect of trade and commerce, including the end of the product's lifecycle. While waste recycling doesn't see as much venture capital thrown at it as say e-commerce, there are some notable companies in this space which have raised funding. For instance, there is Noida-based Attero Recycling, which mines precious metals from e-waste that raised Rs. 100 crore in 2014. Another firm is Karma Recycling, which raised an undisclosed sum during its Series A in July this year. The company buys old gadgets from Tier 1 cities and resells them in India's smaller cities. On its website, it boasts a total payout of 15 crores to its customers.
